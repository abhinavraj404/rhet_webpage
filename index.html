Before researching this topic, I thought of data centers as just facilities that hosted the internet — emails, YouTube videos — without ever considering their environmental impact. That changed.

Technical solutions alone cannot meaningfully reduce the environmental harm of data centers because of Jevons' Paradox. Governments need to pair them with three specific regulatory measures — energy consumption caps, mandatory environmental reporting, and stricter siting standards — to close the gap between efficiency gains and actual energy reduction.

01 — The Scale

A machine the size of a country.

Data centers are large facilities that house servers, networking equipment, and storage systems to process and store the data and models that AI tools use. We have trillion-dollar companies like Google, Amazon, Microsoft, OpenAI, and Anthropic running "hyperscale" data centers that consume hundreds of megawatts of power — almost equivalent to a medium-sized power plant.

The energy required to power a single large data center can serve tens of thousands of homes. Data centers in the United States alone account for approximately two percent of the country's total electricity consumption — and it is only getting worse as AI models become more complex and require significantly more computational power to train and run than even a few years ago.

No one predicted this much growth. Tools like ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, Grok, Deepseek, AWS, iCloud, and streaming platforms have emerged drastically and are now part of everyday life. In February 2026, ChatGPT alone recorded 900 million weekly users.

The AI multiplier
AI models have become more complex and require significantly more computational power to train and run than they did even a few years ago. This isn't a niche infrastructure problem — it's a civilizational-scale resource question dressed up in server racks.
"Data centers accounted for around 1.5% of the world's total energy consumption in 2024 — roughly equivalent to the annual consumption of a country like Japan or Australia." — International Energy Agency, Energy and AI (2025)
02 — Jevons' Paradox

The rebound effect that swallows every efficiency gain.

The standard industry response to criticism is: we're getting more efficient. And it's true. PUE ratios have improved, chip performance per watt has improved, cooling systems have improved. The problem is that none of this has reduced total energy consumption.

This is Jevons' Paradox: when you make something use less energy, it costs less to use, which makes people want to use it more — and this can actually lead to more energy use, not less. It goes all the way back to the 1800s, when coal and steam engines became efficient enough that Britain didn't use less coal — it used vastly more.

There's a new chip with faster clock speeds or a new cooling system every year. It makes computing a little cheaper. That lower cost allows new applications and more users, which drives demand right back up. Fich et al. confirm that "the rebound effect poses a significant challenge to sustainability policies" because focusing only on efficiency improvements while ignoring demand may make matters worse, not better.

What I find particularly interesting is that Guitart himself recognizes this — he warns that energy efficiency could lead to higher workload demand because of lower energy costs — but his strategy never proposes regulatory solutions. Scholars identify the problem. None of them propose how to regulate the demand driving it.

03 — Real Impact

The bill lands on the water, the air, and the community.

Water. Data centers rely on water-intensive cooling systems that use millions of gallons every year. Once water runs through these cooling systems, it is often returned to the environment as heated wastewater, evaporated entirely through cooling towers, or contaminated with chemicals like biocides used to keep algae and bacteria from clogging equipment.

Even though water is technically renewable, water consumed by data centers is effectively removed from the local supply for a long time. In areas already dealing with drought and water scarcity, this creates significant problems for local communities that need water for drinking, bathing, agriculture, and irrigation.

Carbon. PUE only tells you how efficiently a data center uses electricity. It says nothing about whether that electricity is clean. A data center can have an excellent PUE and still be draining a regional aquifer or running on a coal-heavy grid. That is a pretty significant gap if the goal is real sustainability and not just better numbers on a report.

Community. I came across documentaries during my research about communities living near data centers that either had heavily contaminated water or no water at all. One thing that struck me was how little the people nearby knew about the facilities before they were built, and how little say they had in the process.

The people who actually do have a say are usually local zoning boards, county commissioners, and state economic development offices — all heavily incentivized to approve these projects because of tax revenue and construction jobs. The residents who actually live next to these sites rarely show up in those decisions in a meaningful way.

The PUE gap
Grochulska-Salak et al. argue that the industry needs to move past PUE as the main metric and start tracking water use, waste heat recovery, embodied carbon, and land use. The metrics that make facilities look good are widely reported. The metrics that reveal harm are not.
04 — What Researchers Say

The technical foundation — and where it stops short.

Rong et al. cover server virtualization, workload consolidation, and better cooling systems — arguing these are effective ways to reduce energy use. Each can meaningfully reduce per-server energy consumption, but their effectiveness is bounded by how much demand the facility faces. Their article treats the environmental problem as something solvable through better engineering.

Guitart takes a similar approach but goes further with a six-step strategy covering IT efficiency, cooling optimization, power supply improvements, renewable energy, energy markets, and customizing all of this to each data center's local conditions. A data center in a cool climate near a hydroelectric grid will benefit from very different optimizations than one in a hot, fossil-fuel-heavy region. I think this is a more complete way of looking at the problem than focusing on one technology at a time.

Grochulska-Salak et al. look at "green and blue infrastructure" — biogas power plants, green roofs, water retention basins, and biodiversity corridors. What I found interesting is that it goes beyond just making the facility itself more efficient and looks at how it interacts with the environment around it.

Across all of these researchers, the path forward is mainly framed as building better and greener data centers, and their work is genuinely valuable. But what they share is a focus on making individual facilities more efficient without addressing what happens when demand grows faster than those improvements can keep up with.

The gap this essay fills
The technical improvements researchers describe are the foundation. But the foundation alone is not enough. It has to be paired with regulation that addresses the demand-side growth that engineering alone cannot constrain.
05 — What Actually Works

Three regulations that close the gap efficiency can't.

Fich et al. point out that very few energy policies address rebound effects, and when they do exist, they tend to underestimate how significant the effect actually is. They suggest economic measures like carbon taxes and cap-and-trade systems — but only if designed carefully enough to actually change the incentives driving demand. This isn't just a technology problem. It is an economic and political one.

01
Energy Consumption Caps
Instead of only encouraging efficiency improvements, governments should impose a strict limit on how much electricity a data center can use — based on the facility's capacity or what the regional grid can actually support. This directly addresses Jevons' Paradox because even if individual servers become more efficient, overall facility energy use remains capped. Ireland provides a clear example: in 2022, EirGrid stopped approving new data center connections in the Dublin area because the grid could not keep up with residential demands alongside data center needs. While not a formal cap, it effectively acted like one and curtailed the unchecked growth Jevons' Paradox predicts.
Demand-side
02
Mandatory Environmental Reporting
Data centers should be required to publicly report total energy use, water consumption, and carbon emissions — not just PUE. Voluntary industry standards usually fail to create real pressure to improve, but mandatory public reporting does. This allows investors, journalists, and the public to compare facilities and identify those that are merely pretending to be environmentally friendly. The EU's revised Energy Efficiency Directive already does this: operators across all 27 member countries must report energy and water usage data annually to an EU database.
Transparency
03
Stricter Siting Standards
Currently, data centers are often built where land is cheap, electricity is accessible, and tax breaks are high — with very little attention to local water supplies or nearby communities. There should be environmental impact assessments before approval, just like we do for refineries, chemical plants, and other major industrial projects. Operators should have to evaluate water usage, grid pressure, noise, and ecosystem disruption before construction. Grochulska-Salak et al.'s proposed KPIs for water use, land use, and biodiversity could provide a baseline for what those assessments should cover. Local water authorities should have veto power over projects that threaten community supply.
Prevention

Not Enough
Counterargument

The market will handle it on its own.

The pushback
Google, Microsoft, and Amazon have heavily invested in renewable energy and made multiple public commitments to become carbon neutral. Why do we need government regulation when the largest companies are already acting voluntarily?
My response
There is a structural problem with relying entirely on voluntary action: it doesn't factor in demand and growth. If a company makes its data centers fifty percent more efficient but builds twice as many facilities to keep up with AI demand, it ends up doubling their total environmental impact. Voluntary commitments are voluntary — companies can quietly push back timelines or revise goals when they become inconvenient. That's exactly what the data shows happening right now.
Enough.
Conclusion

The technology won't save us. Policy will.

When I started researching this topic, I thought data centers were just facilities that supported the internet. Learning about Jevons' Paradox — and seeing how communities near these facilities are actually affected — changed how I thought about the whole problem.

The technical solutions that Rong et al., Guitart, and Grochulska-Salak et al. propose are necessary but aren't enough on their own because of the rebound effect that Fich et al. describe. As long as making computing more efficient makes it cheaper, and cheaper makes it more abundant, those gains will be outpaced by new demand.

The data center industry can be made sustainable only if we are willing to pair better technology with smarter regulation.
